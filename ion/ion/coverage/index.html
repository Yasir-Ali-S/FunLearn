Ionic Double
Displacement

When two ionic compounds meet, their ions swap partners — forming entirely new compounds. Let's see how and why.

Section 1

What Are Ionic Compounds?

+

Cation (Positive Ion)

An atom that loses electrons, becoming positively charged. Metals typically form cations.

Pb²⁺ · Na⁺ · Ca²⁺
−

Anion (Negative Ion)

An atom that gains electrons, becoming negatively charged. Non-metals typically form anions.

Br⁻ · Cl⁻ · NO₃⁻
i

Ionic compounds form when cations and anions attract each other electrostatically — opposite charges attract, creating a stable crystal lattice.

The Rule

In a double displacement reaction: AB + CD → AD + CB

The cations swap their anions — or equivalently, the anions swap their cations.

⚠ Exception 1

When No Precipitate Forms — No Reaction

NaNO₃(aq) + KCl(aq)
→
Still soluble — NR (No Reaction)

Solubility Rule

A double displacement reaction only proceeds visibly if one product is:

  • Insoluble (precipitate ↓)
  • A gas (↑)
  • Weakly ionized (like water)

If all products are soluble, no net reaction occurs — the ions simply stay dissolved.

NaNO₃ + KCl → KNO₃ + NaCl

All four compounds are soluble → No Reaction (NR)

⚠ Exception 2

Gas-Forming Reactions

Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl
→
2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑

Gas-Forming Rule

When a carbonate or bicarbonate reacts with an acid:

  • H₂CO₃ forms initially
  • It immediately decomposes: H₂CO₃ → H₂O + CO₂↑
  • Effervescence (bubbling) is visible
Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂↑

The driving force here is gas evolution, not a precipitate.
